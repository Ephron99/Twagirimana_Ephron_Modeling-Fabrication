    Materials & Fabrication Methods

    1. Core Philosophy: Designing Material Processes

    In modern engineering, materials are not just components; they are the foundation of design. The key takeaway from our coursework is that we do not design objects—we design material processes.

    Materials as Design Drivers

    Materials are never neutral. They dictate form, influence structural integrity, and define the fabrication logic. A design must align the material + process for optimal results.

    2. Material Intelligence

    Selecting the right material requires understanding its inherent properties and limitations.

    Wood - Nature's Versatile Material
    • Properties: Renewable, distinctive grain patterns.
    • Critical Constraint: Wood is anisotropic—it behaves differently along and across the grain. This directionality affects how it bends and breaks.
    • Best For: Furniture, architectural models, rapid prototyping.
    • Fabrication: CNC Routing, Laser Cutting.
    Plastics - Lightweight & Adaptable
    • Types: Thermoplastics (PLA, ABS) and Thermosets.
    • Best For: 3D printing, laser cutting.
    Heat Sensitivity

    Heat significantly affects plastics, often more than their geometric form. Watch for melting or warping during fabrication.

    Metals - Strength & Precision
    • Common Types: Aluminum (lightweight), Steel (high strength), Brass (conductive).
    • Trade-off: High strength typically comes with increased cost and energy consumption.
    • Fabrication: CNC Milling (subtractive), Sheet Metal Forming.
    Composites - Engineered Performance
    • Definition: Combining multiple materials (e.g., Fiberglass, Carbon Fiber) to achieve properties not found in individual components.
    • Benefit: Extreme strength-to-weight ratio.
    • Challenge: Complex recycling and fabrication methods.

    3. The Fabrication Continuum

    We categorize fabrication into two fundamental approaches:

    A. Subtractive vs. Additive

    Feature Subtractive Manufacturing Additive Manufacturing
    Process Removing material from a block Building objects layer-by-layer
    Examples CNC Milling, Laser Cutting 3D Printing
    Strengths Precision and surface finish Complexity and customization

    B. Hybrid Fabrication

    Advanced manufacturing often combines these methods. For example, using CNC machining to refine a 3D printed part. This "Cross-Process Planning" leverages the strengths of both machines to accelerate prototyping.

    4. Design Logic & Constraints

    Material Form Factors

    • Sheet Materials: (Plywood, Acrylic) Ideal for 2D/2.5D processes like laser cutting. This promotes "Assembly Thinking" (interlocking parts).
    • Solid Stock: (Blocks, Bars) Used for structural components and subtractive 3D forms.

    Forces at Play

    During fabrication, materials react to five external forces:

    1. Compression Pushing
    2. Tension Pulling/Stretching
    3. Shear Sliding
    4. Torsion Twisting
    5. Bending Flexing
    Designing for Assembly

    Assembly is often the core fabrication method itself. Using smart joinery (like Box Joints, Dovetails, or Press-fits) can reduce the need for adhesives and fasteners.

    5. Learning from Failure

    In our lab, failure is treated as data, not a mistake. Common failure modes include:

    • Warping: Signals uneven cooling or internal stress.
    • Cracking: Indicates excessive stress or brittle material properties.
    • Delamination: Suggests poor adhesion between layers (common in 3D printing).

    6. Ethics & Sustainability

    Every fabrication choice carries an ethical weight:

    • Local Availability: Choosing materials found in the immediate region reduces transportation impact and fosters community resilience.
    • Repairability: Designing with common materials ensures products can be repaired locally, extending their lifespan.
    • Efficiency: Can this design do more with less material?
